To Reduce Gun Violence, Strenghtening Gun Regulations May Not Be the Way to Go

By Ashley Hackett

Scroll Down

Illinois: A case study

Illinois authorities confiscated and traced over 10 thousand firearms involved in Illinois crimes in 2017. Of those traced guns, nearly 50 percent were purchased outside state lines, according to the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives (ATF).

Gun regulations in Illinois have become more strict over the past five years as the state attempts to lower its homicide rate, but perhaps policymakers should look at something different: Most crimes that involve guns are not homicides.

Homicide is a small proportion of gun crime

When a gun is found in connection with a crime, law enforcement officers use the gun's serial numbers to trace it back to the original point of purchase. This chart shows the percentage of guns traced from crimes committed in Illinois.



Homicide only accounts for around 1.5 percent of gun-related crimes in Illinois, according to data collected by the ATF. Tightening gun regulations to reduce homicide rates may not be the best approach in the first place – in fact, the number of gun crimes in Illinois is increasing year over year.

Fewer guns used in Illinois crimes are being purchased within the state. As Illinois toughens its gun laws, it’s simply changing the purchase point of the weapons, and not changing the state’s crime levels.



As for the 2014 drop in Illinois-purchased guns, experts don’t quite know what to make of it. Michael Coates, a doctoral candidate at the University of Maryland, coauthored a 2017 study examining state gun control policies. Coates wrote that guns that end up in crime scenes are "more likely to be purchased in states with less regulatory barriers when a state increases the stringency of its gun control laws," so the supply market will shift out of state.

Coates couldn’t think of a state policy that could cause such a drastic fall in the proportion of Illinois crime guns purchased in Illinois. "The trace data is only available for those guns the police chose to trace, so an increase in scrutiny from law enforcement could correlate with an increase to traces of the kinds of weapons that tend to be trafficked,” he wrote in an email. “Some likely factors might be an overall increase in the number of guns being collected and traced within Chicago (Most of the guns traced from Chicago come from Indiana). It could also be the result of a crackdown on gun trafficking – in 2012 a major gun trafficker was arrested in Chicago.

If gun control policies and firearm regulations aren’t reducing crime or homicide, perhaps it’s time to look in another direction. Andrew Papachristos, a Northwestern sociology professor who specializes in the study of gun violence, police misconduct, and illegal gun markets, has taken the idea of gun violence and treated it as an epidemic, finding that the most poignant factor in the violence of big cities is the network in which guns travel.

“What my colleagues and I have tried to do is figure out how developments and network science can help us understand gun violence,” Papachristos said. “We know that gun violence concentrates – that it happens more likely in groups, that you need access to a firearm. So you and I are arrested together, we have a tie, and if you’re arrested with someone else you’ll have a tie to that person. It creates these chains and clusters and clumps and networks of risky behavior. It’s the same conceptual and empirical approach people look at when they’re talking about needle sharing.”

In Papachristos’ study, “Modeling Contagion Through Social Networks to Explain and Predict Gunshot Violence in Chicago, 2006 to 2014,” he found that “social contagion” accounted for 63.1% of the gunshot violence episodes in the 2006 to 2014 time period.

“Gunshot violence follows an epidemic-like process of social contagion that is transmitted through networks of people by social interactions,” Papachristos and his coauthors wrote. “Violence prevention efforts that account for social contagion, in addition to demographics, have the potential to prevent more shootings.”

Papachristos' contagion theory is just one way to approach the rise in Illinois gun violence, but a novel approach is necessary for policymakers who want to see change.
